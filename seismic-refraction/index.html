Edmonton's subsurface carries the signature of the Laurentide Ice Sheet, with interbedded glacial tills and glaciolacustrine deposits masking the underlying Upper Cretaceous bedrock. The depth to the Bearpaw Formation or Horseshoe Canyon Formation can shift from 5 meters to over 30 meters within a single city block, a variation that standard borehole programs struggle to resolve economically. Seismic tomography provides the continuous velocity cross-section needed to map these transitions, distinguishing stiff till from weak shale and identifying buried channels that compromise foundation performance. When integrated with the MASW method for near-surface Vs30 profiling under NBCC 2020, the combined dataset gives structural engineers the confidence to refine site class assignments and optimize deep foundation layouts without excessive conservatism.

A seismic velocity tomogram resolves lithological boundaries at half-meter resolution, turning a handful of borehole logs into a continuous subsurface model that reduces foundation unknowns.

Scope of work in Edmonton

In Edmonton, many times we observe that crosshole seismic tomography becomes the definitive tool when a project encounters a paleovalley filled with soft Lake Edmonton sediments directly beneath a proposed tower footprint. The method generates a high-resolution P-wave velocity image between two or more cased boreholes, revealing low-velocity zones that correspond to normally consolidated clay or loose sand lenses. Interpretation follows ASTM D4428/D4428M guidelines for crosshole testing, with travel-time inversion yielding a 2D tomogram that distinguishes lithological boundaries at a resolution of 0.5 meters or better. Refraction tomography, applied along surface arrays up to 200 meters in length, maps the bedrock surface beneath commercial developments in areas like the Anthony Henday corridor, where rapid lateral changes in overburden thickness are common. The velocity models are calibrated against test pit observations and SPT drilling logs to ground-truth the geophysical contacts, ensuring that the interpreted bedrock surface corresponds to material with an N-value above 50 rather than a dense till layer.

Typical technical challenges in Edmonton

A 14-story residential project in the Oliver neighborhood encountered a 6-meter drop in bedrock elevation across the site width, a feature missed by five vertical boreholes on a regular grid. The undetected trough, filled with soft glaciolacustrine clay, would have imposed differential settlements exceeding 40 millimeters under the proposed raft foundation. Crosshole tomography between two strategically positioned boreholes mapped the trough geometry in two days, allowing the geotechnical team to specify variable-length driven piles that bridged the soft zone and seated in competent shale. The cost of the tomography represented less than 0.3 percent of the total foundation package, a negligible investment compared to the repair liability it eliminated. For infrastructure corridors crossing the North Saskatchewan River valley, refraction tomography also screens for abandoned coal mine workings in the Cretaceous strata, a legacy hazard in parts of central Edmonton where historical mining extends beneath developed neighborhoods.

Our services

The seismic tomography surveys we coordinate in Edmonton address the specific subsurface challenges of glacial terrain: bedrock mapping, paleovalley detection, and dynamic property measurement for seismic design. Each survey is designed around the project geometry and the target depth of investigation.

Crosshole Seismic Tomography

High-resolution P- and S-wave velocity imaging between cased boreholes. Delivers tomograms with 0.5-meter pixel resolution for detecting soft clay lenses, mapping bedrock topography, and deriving small-strain elastic moduli for finite element settlement analysis.

Seismic Refraction Tomography

Surface-based method for profiling overburden thickness and bedrock rippability along linear corridors. Effective for highway widening projects, pipeline routing, and preliminary site characterization where access for drilling is limited.

Downhole and PS Suspension Logging

Borehole velocity measurements for direct Vs and Vp profiles with depth. Used to calibrate surface geophysics and provide the interval velocities required for site-specific response analysis per NBCC 2020.

Frequently asked questions

What is the typical cost range for a crosshole seismic tomography survey in Edmonton?

For two-borehole crosshole tomography surveys in the Edmonton area, the cost typically ranges from CA$3,550 to CA$6,940 depending on the borehole depth, spacing, and number of source-receiver positions required. A site-specific proposal is prepared after reviewing the geotechnical investigation plan and target investigation depth.

How does seismic tomography compare to standard borehole drilling for bedrock mapping?

Boreholes provide precise data at discrete points, while seismic tomography interpolates the subsurface between those points using measured velocity fields. In Edmonton's glacial terrain, where the bedrock surface can vary several meters over short distances, combining three or four boreholes with a crosshole tomogram yields a far more complete picture than drilling alone. The tomography detects intermediate-velocity layers such as dense till that might be mistaken for weathered bedrock in cuttings, preventing overestimation of bearing capacity.

Can seismic tomography detect voids or old mine workings in Edmonton?

Refraction tomography and crosshole surveys are effective for detecting air-filled or collapsed mine workings in the Cretaceous coal seams beneath parts of central Edmonton. The velocity contrast between intact rock and a void or rubble zone is significant, producing a clear low-velocity anomaly on the tomogram. The method has been used successfully on several redevelopment sites where historical mining maps were incomplete or unavailable.
